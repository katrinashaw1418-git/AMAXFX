For a remittance/FX transfer business like AMAX Global, here's what I'd recommend:

Choose "Custom payment flow"

Here's why each option doesn't fit as well:

Shareable payment links — designed for simple product/service sales. Remittance involves variable amounts, recipient details, FX rates, and compliance data collection. Too basic.
Prebuilt checkout form (Stripe Checkout) — better, but you get limited control over the UI. You can't easily capture remittance-specific fields (recipient bank details, destination country, purpose of transfer) or display live FX rates inline.
Why Custom payment flow is right for remittance:

Variable send/receive amounts — you need to show the FX calculation dynamically as the customer types
Compliance fields — remittance requires collecting sender/recipient identity, purpose of funds, etc. You need full form control
Multi-step flow — typically: enter amount → see FX rate → enter recipient details → confirm → pay. A custom flow handles this naturally
Stripe Elements — the underlying UI components (card input, etc.) are still PCI-compliant, but you control the surrounding experience completely
Practically, this means using Stripe Elements (specifically the Payment Element or Card Element) embedded in your React/Next.js frontend, with a server-side Payment Intent created via the Stripe API when the user confirms the transaction.

For a remittance business, here's the honest answer:

Don't use Stripe's product pricing for the transfer amount at all.

Here's why and what to do instead:

How remittance businesses typically charge:

Option 1 — Flat fee (simplest) e.g. $5 AUD per transfer, regardless of amount sent

Option 2 — Percentage fee e.g. 1.5% of the send amount — but Stripe's product setup doesn't do "charge X% of whatever the customer enters." You'd have to calculate it in your code.

Option 3 — FX spread (most common for remittance) You buy USD/PHP/etc. at the real rate, but quote the customer a slightly worse rate. Your margin is baked into the exchange rate, not shown as a fee. Zero visible charge.

Option 4 — Flat fee + spread combined Most competitive remittance businesses (like Wise, Western Union) do this.

What to actually do in Stripe:

Skip setting a fixed price in the product dashboard. Instead:

Customer enters send amount on your site
Your backend calculates: send amount + fee (flat or %)
Create a Payment Intent via Stripe API for that exact calculated total in AUD
Customer pays that dynamic amount
The Stripe "product" just sits in the background for reporting.

For AMAX starting out, I'd suggest a simple flat fee of $4–$6 AUD per transfer. Easy to explain, easy to implement, no complex percentage logic needed upfront.

Yes, absolutely — Option 4 is the right choice for AMAX. Here's the full picture to help you decide:

Why Option 4 makes sense for AMAX

The flat fee covers your fixed costs:

Stripe processing fee (~1.7% + $0.30 AUD for domestic cards)
Your operational cost per transaction
Gives customers a clear, visible charge they expect
The FX spread is your actual profit margin:

You quote customer a rate slightly worse than the mid-market rate
e.g. Mid-market AUD/PHP = 35.00, you quote 34.20
On a $1,000 AUD transfer that's ~$80 AUD margin, invisible to the customer
What a typical AMAX transaction would look like:

Item	Example
Customer sends	$1,000 AUD
Your flat fee	$4.99 AUD
Stripe takes	~$17.30 AUD
Customer total	$1,004.99 AUD
Customer receives	PHP 34,200 (not 35,000)
Your FX spread profit	~$80 AUD equivalent
The honest risk to know upfront:

FX rates move constantly. If you quote a rate and the customer takes 10 minutes to pay, the rate may have shifted against you. You'll need to either:

Lock the rate for a short window (e.g. 10 minutes), or
Refresh the rate if the session expires before payment

Yes! Those keys are how your website/app talks to Stripe. Here's what they are and what to do with them:

Public Key (Publishable Key)

Starts with pk_test_... (test) or pk_live_... (live)
Goes in your frontend code (React, HTML etc.)
Safe to expose — it can't process charges alone
Used to: load Stripe Elements (the card input form)
Secret Key

Starts with sk_test_... (test) or sk_live_... (live)
Goes in your backend/server only (Node.js, Python etc.)
Never expose this publicly — anyone with this key can charge cards and access your Stripe account
Used to: create Payment Intents, confirm payments
Where do you put them?

In your project, create a .env file:

STRIPE_PUBLIC_KEY=pk_test_xxxxxxxxxxxxxxxx
STRIPE_SECRET_KEY=sk_test_xxxxxxxxxxxxxxxx
The basic flow for AMAX:

Customer enters amount + details
        ↓

        ↓
Backend uses SECRET KEY → creates Payment Intent → returns client_secret
        ↓
Frontend uses PUBLIC KEY → Stripe Elements → customer enters card
        ↓
Payment confirmed → Stripe webhook notifies your backend

For AMAX Global FX Remittance — no, you do not need to register tax on Stripe.

Here's why:

Money transfer / remittance services are INPUT-TAXED under Australian GST law

Financial services including currency exchange and remittance are not subject to GST
You do not charge GST to your customers on the transfer fee
This is under Division 40 of the GST Act — financial supplies are input taxed
So in Stripe:

Leave tax registration off
Do not enable Stripe Tax for your transfer fee product
Do not add any tax rate to your $4.99 fee
What this means practically:

Answer
Charge GST on $4.99 fee?	❌ No
Charge GST on FX spread?	❌ No
Register for Stripe Tax?	❌ Not needed
Still need an ABN?	✅ Yes
Still lodge BAS?	✅ Yes, but reporting input-taxed supplies
One caveat — confirm this with your accountant since AMAX may have other taxable supplies depending on your full service offering. But for pure remittance, you're input-taxed.
