KYC and Compliance: Full Gap Analysis for AMAX

1. KYC — Customer Identification Is No Longer Sufficient on Its Own
Status: 🔴 The document's KYC description is materially incomplete

The old framework was called ACIP (Applicable Customer Identification Procedures) — collect ID, verify it, done. The new framework replaces this with a two-phase structure:

Initial CDD and Ongoing CDD are now formally separated, and both are mandatory from the outset for any newly regulated entity. Before providing any designated services, AMAX must both determine the customer's ML/TF risk rating based on factors in its risk assessment, and collect and verify the KYC information required to manage that risk — this generally means determining where the customer fits in the ML/TF risk assessment based on factors like the nature of the customer, their beneficial owners, and the services being requested. 
AUSTRAC

"Assigning a risk profile" as the document described it is not a casual step — it is now a formal, documented, outcomes-focused exercise that drives what KYC you collect and how you verify it.

2. Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) — Mandatory in Specific Circumstances, Not Optional
Status: 🔴 Not mentioned at all in the original document

EDD is not discretionary. AMAX must apply enhanced CDD when the ML/TF risk of a customer is high, and in specified circumstances — for example when the customer is a foreign politically exposed person (PEP), or when a suspicious matter reporting obligation arises in relation to their conduct. 
AUSTRAC

For an eWallet, FX, and crypto FX product, the trigger rate for EDD will be high. Specific mandatory EDD triggers include:

Any foreign PEP as customer, beneficial owner, or beneficiary
High-risk jurisdiction exposure
Shell companies or complex trust/corporate structures with obscured beneficial ownership
Unusually large or complex transactions
Transactions with no apparent economic or legal purpose
Customers who are nested services (i.e. another financial institution's customers flowing through AMAX)
AMAX must apply enhanced CDD when the ML/TF risk of the customer is high and in specified circumstances, including when the customer is a foreign PEP or a suspicious matter reporting obligation arises in relation to their conduct. 
AUSTRAC

3. Source of Funds and Source of Wealth — Now a Mandatory Check in High-Risk Scenarios
Status: 🔴 Entirely absent from the original document

AMAX must establish on reasonable grounds the source of funds and source of wealth of any customer whose ML/TF risk is high and where it is relevant to that risk — including customers involved with high-risk jurisdictions, use of shell companies or complex structures, high-net-worth individuals with unclear income sources, or customers who want to conduct unusually large cash transactions. 
AUSTRAC

For crypto FX and remittance specifically, nearly every cross-border or high-value transaction will require AMAX to be able to demonstrate it assessed source of funds. AUSTRAC requires source of funds and source of wealth checks when AMAX is suspicious that money involved may be derived from criminal activity, if the service has no apparent economic or legal purpose, or if the transaction is unusually complex or involves an unusual pattern. 
AUSTRAC

AMAX must review, and where appropriate update and reverify, customer KYC information at a frequency appropriate to their ML/TF risk. AUSTRAC

Ongoing CDD obligations that apply from 31 March 2026 with no transitional relief include continuous monitoring for changes in customer ML/TF risk, re-verification when KYC adequacy is doubted, and updating source of funds/wealth where circumstances change.

Status: 🔴 The document described metadata capture. AUSTRAC requires a full risk-based monitoring system.

The most common misconception is that filing Threshold Transaction Reports when cash reaches $10,000 AUD is the primary monitoring obligation. It is not. TTRs are the floor, not the ceiling. AUSTRAC's genuine expectation — backed by enforcement action against some of Australia's largest financial institutions — is a continuously operating, risk-based monitoring system that detects suspicious behaviour across the full range of transaction types, amounts, corridors, and customer profiles. 
RemitSo

For AMAX specifically, given it operates cross-border remittance and crypto FX, every cross-border electronic transfer — whether outbound from Australia or inbound — must be reported to AUSTRAC within ten business days, and AUSTRAC mines this data extensively for financial intelligence to identify emerging ML/TF patterns in the Australian cross-border payments market.

6. Reporting Obligations — Four Separate Report Types, All Mandatory
Status: 🔴 Original document mentioned only "AML monitoring" with no reference to mandatory reporting obligations

AMAX will have four mandatory reporting streams:

The reports AMAX must submit include: Suspicious Matter Reports (SMRs) if it suspects a person isn't who they claim to be or has information relevant to criminal activity; Threshold Transaction Reports (TTRs) for any transaction involving physical currency of $10,000 or more; International Funds Transfer Instruction reports (IFTIs) for instructions to transfer funds into or out of Australia; and annual compliance reports. 
AUSTRAC

On SMRs specifically, the timing obligation is strict: an SMR must be filed within three business days of forming a suspicion — not three days after a compliance review confirms the suspicion, and not three days after the transaction settles.
