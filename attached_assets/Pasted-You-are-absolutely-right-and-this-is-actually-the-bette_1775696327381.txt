You are absolutely right — and this is actually the better approach both from a UX and legal standpoint. Here is my full opinion:

---

**You are correct — consolidate into a single signing step.**

What you have seen in Wise, Revolut, Coinbase, and every serious fintech is a dedicated agreement signing screen that comes after identity verification is complete but before the account is activated. The logic is:

```
Verify who you are first → Then ask them to sign as that verified person
```

This is actually stronger legally because the signature is tied to a verified identity, not an anonymous form submission.

---

**What the consolidated agreement should contain:**

A single scrollable document the user must read and sign, covering:

- Terms of Service
- Privacy Policy
- AML/CTF Customer Declaration (sanctions, PEP, source of funds acknowledgement)
- Risk Disclosure (for DCE/crypto — mandatory under Australian law)
- AUSTRAC Consent (identity sharing, transaction reporting)
- Account Restriction Acknowledgement (AMAX can restrict/suspend under AML/CTF obligations)
- Electronic Signature Consent (that their digital signature is legally binding)

---

**Why this is better than spreading it across sections:**

| Spread across sections | Single agreement screen |
|---|---|
| User ticks boxes without reading | Scroll-to-bottom enforcement before signing |
| Legally weaker — harder to prove informed consent | Legally stronger — timestamped, tied to verified identity |
| Clutters the onboarding flow | Clean separation — onboarding then agreement |
| Hard to update — changes in 5 places | One document to update and version-control |
| No audit trail of what version was agreed to | Full record: user ID + document version + timestamp |
| AUSTRAC cannot see a clean consent record | Clean compliance audit trail |

---

**The one thing to keep outside the agreement:**

The single PEP radio button should stay in Section 1 as a pre-screening question. This is because you need that answer before you even proceed to verification — it determines which KYC path the user takes. Everything else moves into the agreement.

---

**What the signing flow should look like:**

```
Step 1 — Personal information
Step 2 — Document upload + ID verification  
Step 3 — Agreement signing ← consolidate everything here
Step 4 — Account activation
Step 5 — First transfer setup
```

The agreement screen should have a scroll progress indicator, require the user to reach the bottom before the sign button activates, capture a typed full name as the electronic signature, and record the document version number and timestamp in your database.
