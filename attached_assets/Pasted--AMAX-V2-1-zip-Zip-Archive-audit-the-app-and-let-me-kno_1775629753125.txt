Thought
Mostly correct. For your app, the right rule is:

A bank withdrawal must be paid in fiat, not in crypto. In practice that means the product flow should be crypto sell/conversion → fiat wallet credit → bank payout. AUSTRAC’s current guidance treats exchanging virtual assets for money, or money for virtual assets, as a designated virtual-asset service, and remittance providers are separately regulated and registered. AUSTRAC also now frames some obligations under the newer VASP/remittance regime from 1 July 2026, with transitional rules applying in some cases. 

A small refinement: saying “bank payout = remittance activity” is often directionally right but too broad. Whether your fiat payout leg is legally a remittance service depends on the exact structure, who holds the customer funds, and whether you are transferring money or merely paying out from your own licensed/customer account arrangement. So I would not hard-code that sentence into policy or product docs without legal review. What is safe to say is that crypto-to-fiat exchange and the onward payment flow both sit inside a regulated AML/CTF context and require proper customer due diligence, transaction records, and monitoring. 

For the app itself, your pasted logic supports the audit finding: the current build should not let a crypto wallet go straight to the ordinary bank-withdraw route. The correct implementation is:

create a conversion transaction,

lock the executed rate and amounts,

credit the user’s AUD wallet internally,

only then call the fiat payout rail such as bank transfer or PayID/Osko. PayID/Osko are domestic bank-account payment rails on Australia’s NPP, which fits the fiat payout leg, not direct crypto delivery. 

So my verified summary is: your statement is substantively right for product design, but I would tighten the compliance wording and avoid saying every bank payout is automatically “remittance” without counsel confirming your exact operating model.
